Fundamental Counting Principle

How to count outcomes when choices happen in steps

What Is the Fundamental Counting Principle?

The Fundamental Counting Principle is used to count how many total outcomes are possible when an event happens in multiple steps.

If one step can happen in a ways, and the next step can happen in b ways, then the total number of outcomes is:

a · b

Every time you move from one choice to another choice, you multiply.

Simple Example

You are choosing:

  • 3 different shirts
  • 2 different pairs of pants

For every shirt, you can wear any pair of pants.

3 · 2 = 6 outfits

Test-Style Example

A student must create a password using:

  • 1 letter (A, B, C)
  • 1 digit (1, 2, 3, 4)

Each letter can be paired with any digit.

3 · 4 = 12 possible passwords

Important Note

The Fundamental Counting Principle does not care about order yet.

It only answers one question:

“How many total outcomes are possible?”

This idea is the foundation for permutations and combinations.

Practice Questions

  1. A menu has 4 appetizers and 5 main dishes. How many different meals can be made?
  2. A lock uses:
    • 2 letters (A–D)
    • 1 number (1–5)
    How many codes are possible?
  3. A student chooses:
    • 1 hoodie from 6 options
    • 1 pair of shoes from 3 options
    • 1 hat from 4 options
    How many outfits are possible?
